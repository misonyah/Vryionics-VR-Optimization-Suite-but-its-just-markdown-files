# Trademark Policy

The MIT license that covers this repository's source code is intentionally permissive — you can fork it, modify it, ship it commercially, even close-source your fork. **What you can't do is reuse the project's name or branding.**

This document is the trademark side of that distinction. It exists because the open-source license and the project's identity are two separate things.

## What's a trademark of Vryionics

The following are trademarks of the project's maintainer:

- **Vryionics** (the name)
- **Vryionics VR Optimization Suite** (the product name)
- **VROS** (the abbreviation)
- The Vryionics logo (`build/icon.png` and `build/icon.ico` in this repo)
- The visual design language of the application UI (color palette, glass-panel layout, ambient orb backgrounds)

## What you can do (under MIT)

- Fork this repository
- Modify the code
- Distribute your fork
- Sell your fork
- Build commercial software that includes this code
- Use the code as a library or component in something larger

The only license requirement is that you preserve the MIT copyright notice in derivative works (`LICENSE` file, copyright header).

## What you can't do (trademark, not license)

- Distribute your fork **as "Vryionics VR Optimization Suite"** or any minor variant (Vryonics, Vryionics VR Suite, VROS, etc.)
- Use the Vryionics logo in your fork's marketing or product UI
- Imply that your fork is endorsed by, sponsored by, or affiliated with the Vryionics project
- Register a domain that confusingly resembles `vryionic.com`
- Publish on GitHub, npm, or any package registry under "vryionics-*" or names that imply affiliation

If you fork and rebrand:

- Pick your own name and logo
- Replace the application icon (`build/icon.*`)
- Update `package.json` `name`, `productName`, all copyright notices
- Make the product visually distinct enough that users won't confuse it with the original

## Nominative use is fine

You can refer to "Vryionics VR Optimization Suite" as the upstream project in your fork's README, changelog, blog posts, comparison tables, etc. You don't need permission to say "this is a fork of Vryionics" or "originally based on Vryionics." Truthful attribution is encouraged and isn't trademark infringement.

## Why a permissive license + trademark restrictions?

Many large OSS projects use this exact split:

- The **Linux** kernel is GPLv2 (modify away) but "Linux" is a registered trademark of Linus Torvalds
- **Mozilla Firefox** is MPL (fork freely) but Firefox + the logo are Mozilla trademarks — that's why Debian shipped "Iceweasel" for years
- **Apache** projects are Apache 2.0 but the project names are Apache Software Foundation marks
- **WordPress** is GPL but "WordPress" is a registered trademark of the WordPress Foundation

This split lets us be genuinely open-source (you can audit, modify, contribute, fork) while preventing the specific failure mode of someone shipping a confusing copy under our name to capture our reputation or our users' trust.

## Enforcement

If you're using this code as the basis for your own work and you've followed the rules above (renamed, re-iconed, made it visually distinct), we'll never bother you. You did exactly what open source is for.

If you ship something called "Vryionics" or wear our logo without being us, we'll send a polite "please change the name" email first. We'd rather avoid courts. But the rights are reserved.
